01 — Business Proposal

The problem with
how NYC inspects restaurants

NYC's Department of Health conducts ~100,000 restaurant inspections per year under a fixed weekly capacity. Scheduling decisions currently don't account for risk — high-risk establishments may go undetected longer than necessary.

"Even a modest improvement in the precision of inspection targeting could meaningfully reduce the lag between when a violation develops and when it is caught."

— PlateRisk Project Proposal, March 2026

📍
The Business Problem

27,000+ active restaurants, ~100,000 inspections per year, fixed weekly capacity K. Currently scheduled by time-since-last-inspection with no systematic risk profiling. High-risk restaurants may wait weeks before being flagged.

🎯
The Use Case

A DOHMH analyst uses PlateRisk's output each week to generate a prioritized inspection queue. The top-K restaurants ranked by predicted risk score are flagged for scheduling — replacing ad hoc scheduling with a data-driven policy.

⚡
The Decision Logic

Prior to each weekly cycle, the model scores all eligible restaurants by predicted probability of critical violation. Top-K are flagged. Below-threshold restaurants are deferred unless they trigger a complaint-based inspection. Human discretion is retained.

💡
Prior Work

Inspired by Chicago's Food Inspection Forecasting project (2016), which used a random forest model and demonstrated high-risk establishments were found ~1 week earlier than under the prior policy. PlateRisk applies this framework to the NYC context.

📈
What the ROC-AUC Tells Us

A ROC-AUC of 0.656 for Random Forest means the model correctly ranks a randomly chosen critical-violation restaurant above a non-critical one 65.6% of the time — meaningfully better than the 50% you'd get from random chance. Despite the high base rate in the dataset, the model is learning genuine signal from features like cumulative past violations, previous score, and days since last inspection.

🏆
Why Random Forest Wins

Random Forest outperforms Gradient Boosting on both metrics at this stage. The top predictive features are total_past_critical (0.218), prev_score (0.179), and days_since_last (0.153) — all historically grounded risk signals that make intuitive sense for a government inspection context.

Borough Fairness Finding
Queens Over-Representation — Reduced

After filtering to initial inspections only, Queens over-representation dropped significantly — from +52.5pp (pre-fix) to +11.9pp under Random Forest. This confirms the earlier extreme over-targeting was largely an artifact of including re-inspections. Some Queens over-representation persists and is flagged as a genuine fairness concern for the final writeup.

Manhattan Under-Representation Persists

Manhattan (34.8% of restaurants) receives only 18.6% of the top-500 inspection slots under the Random Forest model — a −18.2pp gap. This likely reflects that Manhattan restaurants historically show lower cumulative violation counts in the training data, causing the model to systematically deprioritize them. Worth investigating as a fairness issue in the final writeup.

ℹ Results are preliminary and subject to revision. Target definition (any critical violation at next inspection, ~87% base rate in inspected sample) is an open question being discussed with Prof. Provost for the final writeup. ROC-AUC of 0.656 confirms genuine predictive signal regardless of target definition.

05 — Limitations & Ethics

Known Issues & Ethical Considerations

Intellectual honesty is core to rigorous data science. We explicitly flag limitations and ethical risks as part of this proof-of-concept study.

Must Fix
Critical Flag Parsing Bug

The current aggregation uses str.contains('critical') which matches "Not Critical" rows, inflating the critical rate to 86% vs the expected 20–25%. Fix: use exact string match. Under investigation by Tiffany.

Acknowledged
Selection Bias

Only previously inspected restaurants have labels in the dataset. Restaurants that have never been inspected (or rarely) cannot be scored by the model. This is a fundamental, unresolvable limitation of the observational data and will be discussed explicitly in the final writeup.

Genuine Finding
Feedback Loop Risk

Frequently inspected areas accumulate more violation labels, which may reinforce the model's tendency to over-target those areas. The Queens over-representation (80.2% of top-K despite 27.7% population share) is likely a manifestation of this. Monitored via our fairness analysis.

Genuine Finding
Geographic Disparate Impact

Both models under-target Manhattan (−29.7pp) and Brooklyn (−20.9pp) relative to their restaurant population shares. This raises fairness concerns for a government deployment context where all boroughs deserve equitable inspection coverage.

Acknowledged
Transparency & Due Process

Restaurant owners affected by algorithmic prioritization have no visibility into why they were selected. Any real-world deployment would require a transparency layer — e.g., explainable predictions that inspectors can communicate to owners.

Future Work
Hyperparameter Tuning

Current models use sensible defaults validated against the test set. GridSearchCV with TimeSeriesSplit could improve performance further. Deferred to final writeup stage to avoid overfitting to the status report timeline.
